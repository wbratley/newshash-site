Ann Widdecombe murder probe: Counter Terrorism Police say veteran politician killed in 'targeted attack'

Article text

Ann Widdecombe murder probe: Counter Terrorism Police say veteran politician killed in 'targeted attack' The attack in Haytor, Devon, on Ann Widdecombe, the former Conservative minister and later Reform UK spokeswoman, has today been described as "targeted" and "brutal" Counter Terrorism Police have confirmed they believe Ann Widdecombe was killed "in a targeted attack". Authorities gave fresh details about the investigation this afternoon, with a senior officer describing the horror as "a brutal attack on a 78-year-old lady in her home". It has not been declared as a "terrorist incident" as yet but the 28-year-old white British man remains under arrest on suspicion of commission, preparation, or instigation of acts of terrorism, as well as on suspicion of murder. Speaking outside Scotland Yard, Assistant Commissioner Laurence Taylor said: "It is clear that this was a targeted attack. "We are still working to understand the extent of any planning or preparation, and the motivation that sits behind that attack. "I don’t want to comment further on that motivation or preparation at this stage of our inquiries." It comes after emerged today Miss Widdecombe, a former Tory minister, defended Nigel Farage in a radio interview minutes before she died. The veteran politician, who later served as Reform UK's Immigration and Justice spokesperson for three years, completed a ten-minute pre-recorded interview with a Christian radio station TWR-UK, via Zoom, last Wednesday. It began at 11.54am and ended at around 12.10pm - just 20 minutes before police believe she was killed. A warrant of further detention has been granted, which means the suspect can now be held for questioning for up to seven days under the Terrorism Act. Once seen never forgotten, your wit, honesty and sincerity is so rare today, you will be sorely missed." Once a suspect has been arrested, criminal proceedings are considered "active" under UK law. From that point, media organisations are subject to strict legal restrictions designed to protect the fairness of any future trial. The law prohibits the publication of material that creates a "substantial risk of serious prejudice or serious impediment" to active criminal proceedings. However, journalists are still permitted to report a limited number of factual details about the case. These can include a suspect's age, gender, general location, the alleged offence and the status of the police investigation. If the case reaches court, the media can generally report what is said in open court unless specific reporting restrictions have been imposed. Police and the Crown Prosecution Service do not usually identify criminal suspects publicly unless they have been charged with an offence. As a result, news organisations generally do not name suspects before charge. The approach follows a 2022 Supreme Court ruling, which held that people under criminal investigation have a reasonable expectation of privacy and should not normally be identified before charges are brought. If a suspect is charged, police will typically release their name, age and home address. These details establish their legal identity and help distinguish them from others who may share the same name.
