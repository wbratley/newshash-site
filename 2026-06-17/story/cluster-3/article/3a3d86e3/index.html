Eton College and its history of educating royalty, prime ministers and Olympians – and now Prince George

Eton College and its history of educating royalty, prime ministers and Olympians – and now Prince George Founded in 1440 by Henry VI, the schoo has educated 20 prime ministers and generations of British and foreign royalty - Bookmark - CommentsGo to comments Eton College, one of Britain’s oldest and most exclusive public schools, has shaped generations of the nation's elite since its founding in 1440 by Henry VI. Its motto, "Floreat Etona" (Let Eton Flourish), adorns its coat of arms, symbolising a legacy that includes 20 prime ministers and numerous British and foreign royals. Distinguished Old Etonians include the Prince of Wales and the Duke of Sussex, political figures David Cameron and Boris Johnson (contemporaries), and actors Eddie Redmayne, Tom Hiddleston, and Hugh Laurie. The late Queen Elizabeth II even took history lessons there from tutor Henry Marten, later knighted on the college steps by King George VI. With annual fees exceeding £63,000, the boarding school maintains its reputation for academic excellence, consistently sending dozens of pupils to Oxford and Cambridge universities each year. Last year, almost all of Eton’s pupils scored grades 7-9 in their GCSEs, while more than three-quarters of the college’s A-Level cohort secured an A or A*. Pupils at the school, which educates around 1,345 boys between the ages of 12 to 19, can choose from up to 28 subjects, including nine modern and classical languages such as Latin and Ancient Greek. Outside the classroom, the school boasts 350 acres of playing fields and offers 25 different sports, including Eton fives, a handball game invented at the College in the late 19th century. An Old Etonian won a medal at every Olympic Games between 1992 and 2016, with alumni representing Team GB in rowing, cycling, sailing and equestrian events. Music and theatre are also integral tenets of life at Eton, with 50 music ensembles and choirs to choose from and around 18 plays staged every year. It is unknown whether Prince George will board at Eton given its proximity to Windsor Great Park, where he lives with his family, though the school does not currently have any day pupils. Every pupil is a member of one of 25 “houses” first, and Eton College second, each containing around 55 boys. Both William and Harry belonged to Manor House during their time at Eton so it is likely George would follow suit. Manor House, which has been used as a boarding house since at least 1723, was also home to the 1st Duke of Wellington, who claimed: “I really believe I owe my spirit of enterprise to here.” Each boy has his own study bedroom but the boys live, study and eat together. When George arrives at Eton, like all new boys, he will need to accustom himself to the uniform, which was first adopted in 1820 in mourning for the death of King George III. The time-warp uniform consists of black tailcoats and waistcoat, pin-stripe trousers, winged collars and a white bow-tie. The school day starts at about 7.30am with breakfast in the boarding house, which is followed an hour later by Chapel. Lessons end at about 5pm on Mondays, Wednesdays and Fridays. Tuesdays and Thursdays are half-days, but pupils also work half-a-day on Saturdays.
