Andy Burnham wins Makerfield by-election with 55% of vote, intensifying pressure on Keir Starmer's Labour leadership

Unbiased summary

Andy Burnham won the Makerfield by-election on 19 June 2026, securing 24,927 votes (54.8%) and a majority of 9,231 over Reform UK's Robert Kenyon (34.5%). Turnout was 59%, six points higher than the 2024 general election. Burnham, formerly Greater Manchester mayor, stood after incumbent MP Josh Simons resigned to allow him to contest the seat. Liberal Democrat and Green candidates ran subdued campaigns, consolidating left-leaning votes behind Burnham. Hard-right party Restore Britain took 7%. In his victory speech, Burnham described the result as a potential 'turning point' and warned Labour it had a 'final chance to change.' Starmer congratulated Burnham but stated he would contest any leadership challenge. Several Cabinet ministers privately urged Starmer to set a departure timetable. Markets saw a modest dip in sterling.

Coverage by outlet
Morning Star left
Angle Frames Burnham's win as a mandate for left-wing structural change and a springboard to remove Starmer, while warning against complacency about Reform and Labour's rightward drift on immigration.
Bias The Morning Star goes significantly beyond the electoral facts to push a hard-left programmatic agenda, calling Burnham a 'political chameleon' and expressing scepticism about his convictions. It omits any acknowledgement of Burnham's broad coalition of voters or his centrist positioning, instead framing the result primarily through the lens of class struggle and 'ruling class' resistance. The piece treats the by-election as a springboard for left pressure on Labour rather than reporting the result neutrally.

Angle Welcomes Burnham's win as proof Labour can defeat Reform under the right leader, but cautions that Burnham's rhetoric must translate into a credible policy programme rather than mere sloganeering.
Bias The Guardian's editorial coverage is broadly accurate on the vote shares and Burnham's coalition of voters, and its on-the-ground reporting is relatively balanced, including voices from across the political spectrum. However, its editorial framing is clearly sympathetic to Burnham's direction of travel while critically probing Starmer, presenting Starmer's claim to have beaten Reform as 'not credible' without fully airing his allies' counter-arguments. The cartoon and editorial tone signal a pro-change, centre-left stance that goes slightly beyond neutral analysis.
