UK seizes Russian shadow fleet tanker in Channel, charges captain with sanctions breach as Starmer announces further measures at G7

Unbiased summary

Royal Marine commandos and National Crime Agency officers conducted a six-hour operation in the early hours of Sunday, boarding and seizing the MV Smyrtos, a sanctioned Russian shadow fleet oil tanker, in the English Channel — the first UK-led interdiction of its kind. The vessel, flagged under Cameroon but deemed stateless by the UK, was carrying Russian crude oil and is now anchored off Weymouth, Dorset. Its captain, Ajay Pant, a 38-year-old Indian national, was charged with breaching UK sanctions and is due in Southampton Magistrates' Court on Tuesday. Twenty-four crew members remain aboard. Transport Secretary Heidi Alexander formally detained the vessel. Separately, at the G7 summit in Évian-les-Bains, France, Prime Minister Keir Starmer announced additional sanctions targeting Russia's shadow fleet and a £210 million export finance deal to supply enriched uranium to Ukraine's nuclear power infrastructure.

Coverage by outlet
The Guardian left
Angle Frames Starmer's G7 announcements as a deliberate effort to regain political footing after a difficult domestic week, while broadly supporting his Ukraine stance.
Bias The Guardian explicitly contextualises Starmer's G7 activity as a response to a 'torrid political week at home,' framing his foreign policy moves as partly politically motivated — an editorial judgement not present in neutral reporting. It provides good factual detail on the uranium deal and sanctions but downplays any criticism of the government's earlier relaxation of sanctions on Russian-refined oil, which The Independent surfaced. Overall, the coverage is supportive of Starmer's direction while adding a domestic political framing lens absent from strictly factual accounts.

The Independent centre-left
Angle Reports the facts while notably highlighting a contradiction between Starmer's current tough stance on Russia and his government's recent relaxation of sanctions on Russian-refined oil.
Bias The Independent is the only outlet to prominently flag that, just weeks before, the UK government quietly relaxed sanctions allowing imports of jet fuel and diesel refined from Russian oil — directly undercutting the narrative of a consistently tough stance on Russia. This is a material fact omitted by all other outlets and represents genuinely important context. However, by leading on this tension, The Independent frames the story with a slightly sceptical edge toward Starmer. The tanker seizure and G7 announcements are accurately reported across its multiple articles with solid factual detail.
