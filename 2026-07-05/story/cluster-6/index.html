Trump officials warn Burnham against appointing Miliband as chancellor over North Sea energy policy concerns

Unbiased summary

Senior officials in the Trump administration have privately warned incoming UK Prime Minister Andy Burnham that appointing Ed Miliband as chancellor would be a 'mistake', according to reports citing The Times. Their concern centres on Miliband's opposition to new North Sea oil and gas drilling licences, which they describe as 'ideologically driven', and his broader renewable energy agenda. Trump officials have also argued that tax revenues from lifting drilling restrictions could help fund defence spending. Burnham, who is set to enter Downing Street on 20 July, has said he has not yet decided on a chancellor, though Miliband is considered a frontrunner. Burnham himself has stated he is 'open' to new North Sea drilling, indicating a policy difference with Miliband. The Trump administration has previously criticised UK net zero policies publicly and privately.

Coverage by outlet
The Guardian left
Angle The Guardian uses the Trump warning as a backdrop to advocate editorially for Miliband's appointment, framing him as the bold, necessary choice for a transformative Burnham government.
Bias The Guardian almost entirely omits the substance of the Trump administration's warning, mentioning it only implicitly via the George Osborne reference, and instead uses the editorial space to champion Miliband's appointment on progressive grounds. It introduces alternative candidates and even floats Miatta Fahnbulleh as a more radical option, steering far from neutral reporting into open political advocacy. The piece includes a second, entirely unrelated editorial about technology and fiction, diluting the news story further and suggesting the outlet is treating this as an opinion platform rather than a news report.

The Independent centre-left
Angle The Independent frames the story primarily around Trump's hostile relationship with UK energy policy and Starmer, contextualising the warning as part of a broader US-UK tension rather than a substantive policy debate.
Bias The Independent accurately reports the core facts — the Trump officials' warning and Miliband's North Sea stance — but devotes considerable space to the anecdote about Trump's windmill and fox comments, which adds colour but risks making the US position appear eccentric rather than substantive. It notes Burnham's openness to new drilling but does not explore the policy divergence between Burnham and Miliband in depth. The framing leans toward portraying Trump's intervention as politically motivated interference rather than examining the legitimate fiscal or diplomatic dimensions raised by US officials.

City AM centre-right
Angle City AM emphasises the economic and business dimensions of the warning, highlighting industry and City concerns about Miliband's energy agenda alongside the Trump administration's objections.
Bias City AM provides the most substantively detailed account of the policy disagreement, specifically noting Miliband's stalling on Shell and Equinor exploration projects and the divergence between Burnham and Miliband on North Sea policy. However, it frames Miliband's record primarily through the lens of business and industry backlash, without offering Miliband's own stated rationale in equal depth — his argument that North Sea drilling creates little economic benefit is mentioned only briefly. The inclusion of Trump calling net zero policies 'stupid' without broader context slightly amplifies the hostile framing toward Miliband's position.
