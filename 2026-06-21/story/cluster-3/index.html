Iran claims Strait of Hormuz closed over Lebanon strikes as US-Iran talks proceed in Switzerland

Unbiased summary

Iran's military announced it was closing the Strait of Hormuz, citing Israeli strikes in southern Lebanon as a violation of a recently signed US-Iran interim agreement. The US military disputed the claim, stating traffic continued to flow and that 55 merchant ships carrying 17 million barrels of oil transited the strait on Saturday. The situation remained ambiguous, with cargo ships anchored in the Gulf of Oman. Despite the announcement, both sides proceeded with negotiations in Bürgenstock, Switzerland, with US Vice President JD Vance and Iranian delegation led by Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi and parliamentary speaker Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf attending. Pakistan and Qatar served as mediators. Israel said its strikes responded to Hezbollah rocket fire and that it remained committed to the ceasefire but would continue operations against threats to Israeli forces.

Coverage by outlet
The Guardian left
Angle Frames the story around diplomatic fragility and the threat the crisis poses to the broader peace process, with emphasis on Trump's unorthodox interventions.
Bias The Guardian highlights Trump's social media post about tolls as a notable and slightly erratic detail, lending it prominence that colours the US response as improvised. It gives relatively balanced space to both the Iranian claims and US denials but front-loads the threat to the peace deal, framing instability as the dominant narrative. The piece appropriately notes uncertainty about whether the closure was real, which is accurate, but the framing subtly casts the overall situation as more precarious than outlets closer to the centre.

BBC News centre-left
Angle Presents the story as a diplomatic proceeding under stress, foregrounding the talks continuing despite the Hormuz claim, with measured treatment of both sides.
Bias The BBC gives roughly equal weight to Iran's announcement and the US denial, and includes Vance's more optimistic framing about Lebanon stabilising, which some outlets omit. It accurately names the Iranian delegation and Pakistan's mediating role with more specificity than most. The piece leans slightly toward normalising the talks as progressing, which is factually supportable but marginally downplays the genuine uncertainty about the strait's status and the severity of the Lebanese strikes.

i Paper centre
Angle Leads with the US rebuttal and positions the story primarily as one of American reassurance and factual denial of Iranian claims.
Bias The i Paper foregrounds US statistics — 55 ships, 17 million barrels — prominently and early, lending the US denial disproportionate weight over Iran's stated grievances. It gives meaningful detail on the Swiss talks and the Iranian delegation's conditions but omits any mention of Israeli casualties in Lebanon or the IDF's justification for strikes, which are relevant context for why Iran acted. The tone is the most procedurally neutral of the outlets but the structural emphasis on US data nudges the framing toward the American position.

City AM centre-right
Angle Frames Iran's closure announcement as a direct and serious response to ceasefire violations, giving Iran's stated rationale relatively full treatment before pivoting to US and Israeli rebuttals.
Bias City AM is notable for quoting the specific MOU clause Iran cited, providing more legal-diplomatic context than most outlets. However, it describes Israeli strikes as 'alleged violations' in framing Iran's position but does not initially challenge this framing with equivalent emphasis, creating a slight early tilt toward legitimising Iran's complaint before the US rebuttal appears. It also presents Israeli strikes as potentially provocative without proportionate early context about Hezbollah's preceding rocket fire, which the IDF cited as justification.

Daily Mail right
Angle Sensationalises the Iranian closure announcement as causing regional 'chaos', while positioning Israel's restraint under US pressure as a positive diplomatic development.
Bias The Daily Mail's headline and opening use the word 'chaos' — an editorial judgment not supported by confirmed facts, as the strait's closure remained unverified and talks continued. It emphasises Netanyahu's reported 'hold fire' order as an 'apparent 11th-hour intervention', framing Israel favourably as responsive to US pressure without noting that Israeli strikes had already killed 16 people by that point. It mentions Iranian casualties and strike details less prominently than pro-ceasefire framing of Israeli behaviour, and the overall tone is more alarmist than the facts at the time warranted.

GB News right
Angle Runs two separate articles that together sensationalise the closure claim while also covering Vance's reassuring statements, creating a dramatic tension that favours the more alarming framing.
Bias GB News published one article headlined with Iran issuing a 'chilling warning', an editorialised characterisation not used by neutral sources. The second article is more procedural but the combined output amplifies alarm disproportionately. Both pieces mention US denials but position them as counterpoints to an assumed closure rather than the primary verified account. The reference to this being the 'first face-to-face meeting since April' adds context but the outlet omits meaningful detail about Hezbollah's rocket attacks that preceded Israeli strikes, presenting Israeli military action with less justifying context than centrist outlets provide.
