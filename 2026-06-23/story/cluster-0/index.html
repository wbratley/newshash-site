Keir Starmer resigns as UK Prime Minister after losing Labour parliamentary support; Andy Burnham expected to succeed him

Unbiased summary

Keir Starmer announced his resignation as UK Prime Minister and Labour leader on Monday 22 June, in an emotional speech outside 10 Downing Street. He acknowledged his parliamentary party did not believe he was best placed to lead Labour into the next general election, and set 9 July as the start of the leadership nomination process, with a successor to be in place before parliament returns in September. His resignation followed Andy Burnham's decisive victory in the Makerfield by-election, which accelerated the collapse of cabinet and MP support for Starmer over the weekend. Wes Streeting, the only other declared candidate, announced he would back Burnham, making a coronation likely. Burnham was sworn in as MP for Makerfield on the same day and confirmed his leadership bid. The UK-EU summit planned for 22 July was subsequently postponed. A red weather warning for extreme heat was also issued for parts of England and Wales.

Coverage by outlet
Morning Star left
Angle Starmer's departure is framed as an overdue and welcomed removal of a failed, morally inferior leader, with Burnham cast as Labour's saviour against the hard right.
Bias The Morning Star uses openly hostile, editorial language toward Starmer, calling him 'hapless', 'self-righteous', 'self-pitying', and 'mean-spirited', none of which appears in objective reporting. It approvingly compares Jeremy Corbyn to Starmer in Corbyn's favour, injecting partisan framing absent from other outlets. It omits any balanced assessment of Starmer's record or the procedural details of the leadership contest, and frames Burnham's rise almost entirely in ideological terms rather than factual ones.

The Guardian left
Angle Starmer's fall is presented as a complex political failure deserving serious analytical scrutiny, with nuanced attention to his record and the historical context of UK political instability.
Bias The Guardian's analytical pieces are among the most substantive and least emotionally driven, examining Starmer's economic record with charts and contextualising his approval ratings historically. However, its letters section publishes highly partisan reader reaction without editorial balance, and its 'rise and fall' analysis, while thoughtful, subtly frames Starmer's removal as puzzling rather than examining internal Labour party dynamics critically. Its coverage of Burnham is broadly positive, describing him as a 'man of the people' with limited critical scrutiny of his record.
