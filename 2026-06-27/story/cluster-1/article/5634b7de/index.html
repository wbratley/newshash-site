Venezuela earthquake live: Dozens of UK search and rescue teams deployed as death toll nears 1,000

Article text

Venezuela earthquake live: Dozens of UK search and rescue teams deployed as death toll nears 1,000 Two powerful earthquakes rocked northern Venezuela on Wednesday night - at least 920 people have been confirmed dead so far, with the figure expected to rise as tens of thousands are missing A British search and rescue team has been deployed to Venezuela as the death toll from the double earthquake has surged to nearly 1,000. The team of 68 - made up of personnel from fire brigades across the country - left RAF Brize Norton on Friday along with six specialist search dogs and humanitarian staff. Prime Minister Keir Starmer said: "My thoughts are with the people of Venezuela following this week's devastating earthquakes. "The UK stands in solidarity with all those affected, particularly those who have lost loved ones, homes and livelihoods." Heartbroken families have taken the search for missing loved ones into their own hands after claiming rescue teams are nowhere to be seen in the wake of two devastating earthquakes. At least 920 people have died and thousands more left injured after Venezuela was rocked by two devastating earthquakes on Wednesday night - with the figure expected to rise significantly as around 50,000 remain missing. Over 8,000 people have been found alive. Footballer Héctor Bello, a defender for second division side Marítimo de La Guaira, lost his wife Andrea when she heroically used her body as a human shield to save her daughter's life. He wrote on social media: "You left us alone in the fight, mummy. You left me all alone with our daughter." Mum-of-three Dayana Delgado, who is searching for her missing 8-year-old son, said: "I want to know where my child is, if he’s trapped or in a shelter." She said she had not seen any of the 'heavy machinery' that authorities had promised - and that she and her neighbours were the ones digging through crumpled buildings. Venezuelan president Delcy Rodriguez today insisted workers are doing all they can to save lives, telling reporters: "We are going to rescue the people who are trapped. We are working tirelessly on this task." A 7.2-magnitude earthquake hit west of Moron on the Caribbean coast, about 105 miles west of the capital Caracas, at around 6pm local time (11pm BST) on Wednesday, the US Geological Survey said. Less than a minute later, a 7.5-magnitude earthquake struck, with its epicentre 10 miles south-west of Moron. Footage posted on social media showed vehicles shaking, smoke filling the streets and buildings badly damaged or collapsed due to the tremors. Key Events US Southern Command “stands with the people of Venezuela” it said after its personnel and the US Chargé d’Affaires in the country met Venezuela’s acting president Delcy Rodríguez. Southern Command, whi ch oversees US military activities in Latin America and the Caribbean, posted a photo of the meeting on X on Friday local time. The US is “rapidly surging critical capabilities to support earthquake relief operations and help save lives,” at the request of Venezuela, it added. President of the National Assembly of Venezuela, Dinorah Figuera, took to X to write: "The earthquakes that have occurred in our country have left irreparable human losses. We extend our solidarity to all the affected families and express our gratitude for the collaboration provided by the government of the United States of America. "We are in direct contact with their authorities and with the Department of State, who are accompanying us during these most painful hours for everyone. God bless Venezuela." Football fans across the globe have reacted with outrage after a minute's silence to honor victims of the devastating Venezuela earthquake was marred by shouts and jeers from the crowd during Friday's World Cup matches. The tributes were held before kick-off in the Group H games between Spain and Uruguay, and Cape Verde against Saudi Arabia, as well as the earlier Group I fixtures. However, the solemn moment was disrupted during the Spain-Uruguay match in Guadalajara by individuals yelling and making noise, prompting an immediate backlash on social media from viewers around the world. "Everyone who yelled during the minute of silence should receive a lifetime ban," one furious fan wrote on Twitter, using the hashtags #Mundial, #WorldCup and #FifaWorldCup. "Do people not know how to have a minute of SILENCE??" A mother displaced by the Venezuelan earthquakes described the turmoil her family faced as they fled their home, fearing it would collapse around them, reports CNN. “My daughters were saying to me, ‘Mom, get me out of here, we do not want to die here,’” Luz Marina Marcano told Reuters on Friday as she sat in a tent with her family. “I did not want to leave my house alone because criminals were breaking into homes… So my partner, who is a public servant, told me to come here and camp with my daughters because they would be safer here.” In the latest update on the aftermath of Wednesday’s earthquakes, the president of Venezuela’s National Assembly, Jorge Rodríguez, said that 13 hospitals were damaged across the country. Venezuela’s acting president, Delcy Rodríguez, announced Wednesday night that she had ordered the entire hospital network, including both public and private facilities, to coordinate efforts to treat the injured. According to the latest figures, 3,360 people were injured in the disaster. The government has also reported at least 920 deaths nationwide, most of them in La Guaira, the hardest-hit state. King Charles and Queen Camilla have posted a message to the people of Venezuela, sending them their "most heartfelt condolences to all those affected by this terrible tragedy". In some of the worst-hit areas like La Guaira and Moron, hospitals are overflowing with the injured. Packed-out wards mean many patients are currently being treated outside on the streets, or in courtyards. Local medical workers are pleading for basic items - such as dressings, gloves, thermometers, and painkillers. Help is on the way for Venezuela tonight - but the days of rescue, recovery and rebuilding ahead are likely to be very testing for what was already a struggling nation. Hospitals are currently dealing with a massive surge in patients while battling disruptions to electricity and water supplies. And severe damage to the Simón Bolívar International Airport and major motorway networks has complicated the delivery of heavy rescue equipment and international aid. The coastal state of La Guaira, the hardest-hit "disaster zone" where more than 100 high-rise flats and buildings collapsed, is now under military control to manage security and aid. Others who survived the earthquake have been able to return home - but damage is widespread, even in buildings left standing. A rapid assessment by the United Nations Development Programme (UNDP) has put the direct economic damage to Venezuela at $6.7billion (£5billion), coming at a time when the country was already dealing with a prolonged financial crisis. Venezuelan authorities have said 861 international volunteers from Mexico, the U.S., El Salvador, Switzerland, Colombia and beyond were working in Venezuela to provide aid and help with the rescue effort.
