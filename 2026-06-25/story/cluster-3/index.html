Study finds 85% of Australian under-16s still using social media three months after ban, raising questions about enforcement effectiveness

Unbiased summary

A University of Newcastle, Australia observational study of 408 young people aged 12–17, published in The BMJ, found that approximately 85% of under-16s reported still using social media platforms three months after Australia's Social Media Minimum Age Act came into force in December 2025. The law prohibits under-16s from holding accounts on platforms including TikTok, Instagram, Facebook, X, YouTube and Snapchat. Researchers concluded there was insufficient evidence of immediate substantive reductions in social media use, citing inadequate age-verification checks and circumvention methods such as fake accounts and private browsing. The study acknowledged the ban is still new and noted it could take a decade to assess full impact. The findings have been cited in debate over the UK's proposed similar ban, announced by Prime Minister Keir Starmer, which is due in spring 2027.

Coverage by outlet
The Guardian left
Angle The Guardian frames the findings as evidence that bans alone are insufficient and that a broader, more holistic strategy is needed to protect children online.
Bias The Guardian accurately reports the study's findings and appropriately notes expert caveats, but it notably emphasises the views of 'experts and campaigners' who argue banning is not enough, steering the reader toward a policy-reform narrative rather than a straightforward news report. It is the only outlet to provide detailed context about the UK's proposed ban, including the specific platforms and gaming sites covered. It largely avoids partisan framing of Starmer but steers coverage toward child safety advocacy perspectives, slightly downplaying the study's own acknowledgement of its early and limited scope.

Angle The Independent frames the story as casting doubt on the UK's incoming restrictions, foregrounding uncertainty and risk without strongly endorsing or attacking the policy.

However, its headline uses the phrase 'fears over UK crackdown,' introducing an emotive framing not directly supported by the study itself. It also accurately notes the study's caveat that a decade may be needed to assess full impact, which other outlets underplay.
