Burnham is forced to review the early prison release scheme after PC Harper's widow blasts soft justice programme

Article text

Burnham is forced to review the early prison release scheme after PC Harper's widow blasts soft justice programme - PAUL BIRCH: Nothing more vividly illustrates the horror of Labour's early release scheme than freeing PC Harper's killers - See more Daily Mail on Google - save us as a Preferred Source The widow of hero PC Andrew Harper last night blasted Labour's soft justice prisoner release scheme as 'deplorable'. Lissie Harper spoke out after it emerged that two of her husband's killers are due to be freed early. She accused politicians of 'making decisions from behind a desk that will have real and lasting consequences for victims' families like mine'. Last night, following her dramatic intervention, the future of the release programme was in doubt. Prime Minister Andy Burnham reiterated a previous vow to review it – a flagship Labour policy to ease prison overcrowding – when questioned by journalists during a visit to Bath. The Ministry of Justice refused to confirm reports that prison governors had been told to pause the scheme. But a source told the Daily Mail that officials have been summoned to an online meeting with members of the Prison and Probation Service today to discuss it. Mrs Harper led senior police, campaigners and MPs in demanding Mr Burnham intervene to stop Jessie Cole and Albert Bowers being released among waves of killers, rapists and violent criminals from September. Lissie Harper, the widow of hero PC Andrew Harper, last night blasted Labour's soft justice prisoner release scheme as 'deplorable' Henry Long, left, Jessie Cole, centre, and Albert Bowers, right, were jailed over PC Harper's death Albert Bowers (left) and Jessie Cole (centre) pictured leaving Reading Magistrates Court in 2019. They are due to be freed under Labour's early release scheme PC Harper was killed in the line of duty in 2019 when he was dragged behind a car for more than a mile as he tried to stop three teenage thugs from stealing a quad bike. His widow fought for Harper's Law – a mandatory life sentence to anyone who kills an emergency worker in the line of duty. But yesterday she said the widespread political support which changed the law in her husband's memory now felt 'forgotten'. In a powerful statement after years spent largely away from the public eye, Mrs Harper said: 'It is deplorable to me that we have reached a point where releasing prisoners early is even considered acceptable.' She added: 'We speak of justice, accountability and standing with victims, yet decisions like this do the opposite. 'They tell victims their suffering matters less, and offenders that the punishment handed down by our courts is no longer what they will actually serve. 'For victims' families, this is not about politics. It is about whether a sentence means what a judge says it means. It is about whether justice is something we truly believe in, or something that can be negotiated away when it becomes inconvenient.' Mrs Harper signed off by saying: 'Andrew deserves better. Every victim deserves better. And the British public deserve a justice system they can have faith in.' PC Andrew Harper and Lissie Harper were married just four weeks before the Thames Valley Police officer was killed in 2019 Pressure on the Prime Minister to act was already mounting after Cabinet ministers Wes Streeting and Shabana Mahmood were caught laughing about the scheme hours before the news about PC Harper's killers emerged. Mr Burnham said their behaviour was not acceptable. Mr Harper, 28, died in horrific circumstances when he was caught in a towing strap between the stolen quad bike and the teenagers' getaway car. He was dragged for more than a mile at 'sickening' speed down winding country lanes near Sulhamstead in Berkshire before, mortally wounded, he was shaken loose, a court heard. His injuries were so grave that those arriving on the scene initially thought they had come across a deer carcass. PC Harper had been due to clock off hours earlier, but decided to respond to one final call before starting his honeymoon with childhood sweetheart Mrs Harper. The pair had been married for only four weeks. Cole and Bowers, who were 17 at the time and were passengers in the car, were handed 13 years in custody for manslaughter after a trial at the Old Bailey in 2020. The judge described the trio as 'unintelligent, but professional criminals', and said they would each serve two-thirds of their sentence. The driver, Henry Long, 18, was handed a life sentence with a minimum term of 16 years. He is ineligible for early release under current Labour plans. Dame Priti Patel, the former Tory home secretary who helped create Harper's Law, said releasing Bowers and Cole early would be 'outrageous'. She added: 'These offenders should be kept behind bars where they belong.' Former Tory justice secretary Robert Buckland KC, who also worked on the legislation with Mrs Harper, told the Mail that early release would be 'an insult' to PC Harper's memory. PC Harper was killed in the line of duty in 2019 when he was dragged behind a car for more than a mile as he tried to stop three teenage thugs from stealing a quad bike Lissie, pictured in 2022, yesterday accused politicians of 'making decisions from behind a desk that will have real and lasting consequences for victims' families like mine' He said: 'It's as if the work that the Harper family did is being forgotten. Why on earth did they think this was appropriate in the first place? 'Andy Burnham needs to pause this coming into effect and focus on [releasing] people who are not violent or sexual offenders who are not a danger to the public.' Reform UK leader Nigel Farage said the early release scheme – part of the Sentencing Act – was 'an utter insult to the victims and any decent people that may be endangered by criminals walking their streets'. Aileen O'Connor, chairman of Thames Valley Police Federation, representing colleagues from PC Harper's constabulary, said: 'Police across Thames Valley – and indeed the country – will be disgusted at the prospect of these smirking lowlifes being free to roam the streets. What message does this send about the worth of a life of a police officer?' And Thames Valley Police Chief Constable Jason Hogg said the prospect of early release 'feels wrong'. He has written to the Prime Minister 'to make my opinion on this clear'. Claire Waxman, the Victims' Commissioner for England and Wales, said: 'As things stand, I do not have confidence that these releases can be managed in a way that fully safeguards victims, protects the public and maintains confidence in the justice system.' Bowers and Cole are among around 6,000 criminals set to benefit from Labour's Sentencing Act, which will allow violent and sexual offenders to be released after serving just half their sentence, rather than the current two-thirds. It means the duo could be released within the year. Other offenders could be freed after serving just a third of their sentence if they demonstrate good behaviour while in jail. Murderers will not be considered. Mr Burnham said: 'I've spoken to the new Justice Secretary [Alex Norris]. We will review the policy together and make further statements in due course.'
