New research finds Reform UK support rooted in distinct socially conservative ideology, not purely protest voting, with electoral implications unclear

Unbiased summary

New polling research, cited by leading polling expert Professor Sir John Curtice, indicates that Reform UK's support base is driven by a distinct socially conservative ideology rather than being primarily a protest vote against other parties. The research also shows Reform is drawing heavily from former Conservative voters. Separately, a poll found that union members are now as likely to support Reform as Labour, with Labour's union support reportedly falling around 20 points since the general election. Curtice's analysis suggests Reform is a genuine ideological movement, though the research also raises questions about whether that same socially conservative focus represents a ceiling on the party's growth potential, given those views are held by a minority of the overall electorate.

Coverage by outlet
The Guardian left
Angle The Guardian frames the research primarily as evidence that Reform UK faces a natural growth ceiling due to its reliance on minority socially conservative views.
Bias The Guardian leads with the limitation angle — the potential plateau — while downplaying the finding that Reform's support is ideologically genuine and substantial. It omits Curtice's striking quote about Reform eating the Conservatives 'for breakfast, lunch and dinner' and does not mention the union member polling data. This framing reassures left-leaning readers that Reform's rise may be self-limiting, which strays from a fully balanced presentation of the findings.

The Independent centre-left
Angle The Independent emphasises that Reform's support is a serious, substantive political force rather than a fleeting protest movement, while also highlighting Labour's vulnerability among union members.
Bias The Independent provides relatively balanced coverage by including both the ideological depth of Reform's support and Labour's declining union base, making it the most comprehensive of the three outlets. However, by highlighting Curtice's colourful 'breakfast, lunch and dinner' quote about Conservative losses, it subtly frames the story around Conservative collapse rather than Reform's own ideological appeal. It does not prominently address the potential growth ceiling identified in the same research.

Daily Mail right
Angle The Daily Mail frames the research as evidence that Reform is a rising ideological force with a credible path to winning the next general election.
Bias The Daily Mail goes furthest from the neutral facts by leading with the claim that Reform winning the next election is 'growing more likely,' a conclusion that significantly extrapolates beyond what the research explicitly states. It omits the finding about Reform's potential support ceiling entirely, presenting only the positive electoral indicators for the party. This selective emphasis reflects the outlet's broadly sympathetic stance toward Reform and its right-leaning readership, making its coverage the most one-sided of the three.
