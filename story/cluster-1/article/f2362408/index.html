I just feel so sorry for Emma Raducanu

Article text

WIMBLEDON — It was the news no one really wanted to read, apart from the most ill-spirited of trolls. For the rest of us, Emma Raducanu pulling out of Wimbledon with a stress fracture late on Sunday night, barely 12 hours before her first-round match against Antonia Ruzic, was the latest blow in a career that has been dogged by injuries. You cannot help but feel sorry for someone who is such an obviously talented player that even missing half of the season, she is still ranked No 32 in the world. And it was made even more tragic that had you bumped into Raducanu on Sunday, as I did, you would never have known that she was about to pull out of the biggest tournament of her season. Apart from the strapping on her shin, the 23-year-old had cut a jovial figure crossing the Aorangi Park practice facility at Wimbledon on Sunday morning, stopping to shake hands and chat with former coach Francis Roig. She cheerily greeted me and other reporters who had gathered to check on her progress, and then laughed her way through a spirited session with coach Andrew Richardson and hitting partner Alexis Canter. If Raducanu was anxious at training on Sunday, she did not show it (Photo: Getty) Photographers have tried hard this week to capture images of the British No 1 looking upset or concerned – not through malice, but just in order to illustrate correctly the stories of worry over her fitness – but they have struggled. Raducanu has actively put a brave face on things when training with her team. “In general we have a great banter between us. It helps lighten the mood,” Raducanu said. ”Laughing always helps. We all get on well, know each other well at this point. So that’s good. “I have one of my best friends just moved over from New Zealand to London. That helps as well. It’s just nice of course, British summertime, everyone is also in a better mood.” At one point during her final practice session, Raducanu sat on the grass and chatted warmly with her agent Jill Smoller. One can only imagine that both of their minds must have been racing, but an uninformed observer would have guessed they were comparing notes on restaurant recommendations or talking about where they should watch the football. Raducanu (left) and Smoller chat at practice on Sunday (Photo: Getty) It was enough to convince those assembled almost unanimously that she would at least give it a go on No 1 Court come Monday lunchtime. “That’s the plan,” Raducanu said in her pre-tournament press conference, just hours before withdrawing. Perhaps it was confirmation bias that I swallowed her bullishness so readily, and ignored that she was unwilling to simply say “yes” to playing. Impartiality is all well and good – but no one sits in the press room rooting for an injury. Especially not when Raducanu’s career has been such a major theme of British tennis, ever since that remarkable summer of 2021: England nearly won the Euros, we all went back to the pub, and a teenager from Bromley, not once but twice. won the hearts of the nation. Her fame has endured in the five years since, but the form that powered her to the fourth round of Wimbledon and then a historic US Open title has not. A litany of injuries, a carousel of coaches and the misguided perception that she would rather be modelling jewellery than playing tennis became the preferred brickbats of her detractors. A girl who should have been the darling of the British public had become a figure of division, through no fault of her own. What an apt summation of the state of the world. It was easy to spin her first-round defeat at the French Open as an opportunity to start her grass-court preparations early, which looked like more than just a coping mechanism when she made the final of Queen’s with back-to-back victories on a condensed Saturday. But then she pulled out of Nottingham the week after and when her name mysterious vanished from the practice schedule on Tuesday, six days out from Wimbledon, alarm bells started to ring. On Wednesday, specialist website Clay reported that she had been spotted in an orthopaedic boot and she did not return to the practice court until Saturday, when strapping at the base of her right shin suggested a new injury, different from previous ankle issues. Then just as there were three matches on that last weekend of Queen’s for Raducanu, so there were three practice sessions on grass this weekend. Both ended in vain, but this different type of defeat at SW19 without having made the start line stung far more. “Playing at Wimbledon, in front of a home crowd, means everything to me,” Raducanu wrote. “This is really difficult to process.” I bet it is, Emma.
