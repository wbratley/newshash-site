Andy Burnham live: New PM to host first cabinet meeting this morning after Donald Trump phone call

Andy Burnham live: New PM to host first cabinet meeting this morning after Donald Trump phone call New Prime Minister, Andy Burnham, has already made a number of sweeping changes to the Cabinet and later this morning he is set to host his first cabinet meeting Andy Burnham made his first Cabinet appointments yesterday — bringing back Angela Rayner to the fold. Talking yesterday without a lectern or notes, in a huge move away from tradition, Mr Burnham, 56, spoke for 10 minutes and used the opportunity to outline his vision for the UK. In his first speech as PM, Burnham said an announcement about tackling the cost of living would be made today. He said he could "do something to give people some breathing space now" and was looking at measures "that could make a difference this year". And confirmed he was "finalising the details" and that some of the measures - including "how we pay for them" - would be set out today. The new Prime Minister will begin his first full day as prime minister with a cabinet meeting this morning. His team includes John Healey as chancellor, Shabana Mahmood as home secretary, and Ed Miliband as foreign secretary. For updates on Andy Burnham becoming prime minister, follow our live blog below Key Events Andy Burnham became Britain's seventh Prime Minister in a decade on Monday and wasted no time getting down to business in the top job. His opening day kicked off with a formal audience with the King at Buckingham Palace before he delivered his maiden address as PM on the steps of No 10. Mr Burnham then acted swiftly to name his new Cabinet. The Makerfield MP, who made his return to Parliament in June following a stint as Mayor of Greater Manchester, was formally elected Leader of the Labour Party on Friday. Andy Burnham has spoken with Antonio Costa, the president of the European Council, one of the EU’s major institutions. Giving a readout of their call, a Downing Street spokeswoman said: “The Prime Minister spoke to Antonio Costa, President of the European Council, this evening. “The Prime Minister began by underlining the importance of creating a stronger Europe through a closer UK-EU relationship. “That included working together on shared challenges such as defence and security and driving growth across the UK and Europe. “They both agreed their respective teams would work closely towards a UK-EU summit later in the year.” Lisa Nandy, who has kept her job as Culture Secretary, told reporters she was “very” happy with her job as she left Downing Street smiling on Monday evening. Andy Burnham has spoken with Mark Rutte, the Nato secretary general, and German Chancellor Friedrich Merz, No 10 said. Giving a readout of the Prime Minister’s call with the Nato chief, a Downing Street spokeswoman said: “The Prime Minister began by congratulating the Secretary General on the successful Nato summit held in Ankara earlier this month, and the strong signal of unity it sent to the world in such volatile times. “Updating on the appointment of the former defence secretary, John Healey, as the new Chancellor of the Exchequer, the Prime Minister said the appointment was a signal of his intent. “The leaders also discussed the situation in Ukraine, and the Secretary General welcomed the Prime Minister’s strong statement of support for Ukraine earlier today.” In a separate readout of the call with Mr Merz, the Downing Street spokeswoman said: “The Prime Minister spoke to the Chancellor of Germany, Friedrich Merz, this evening. It was clear the relationship between the UK and Germany was vital to delivering for people in both countries, the Prime Minister said. “Both leaders underlined their ambition to increase cooperation under the Kensington Treaty, and the Prime Minister said he would ask his teams to accelerate work on implementation. “Discussing Ukraine, the Prime Minister looked forward to working with Chancellor Merz and President Macron as they drove the Coalition of the Willing forward to support a just and lasting peace.” Andy Burnham has announced a major change in personnel at the top of government hours after becoming Prime Minister. Chancellor Rachel Reeves was among the high-profile names axed as the new PM installed his top team - with John Healey named as her surprise replacement. Shabana Mahmood remains Home Secretary, while Ed Miliband was named as Foreign Secretary. And there was a return to government for Angela Rayner less than a year after she stepped down as Deputy PM. Other high-profile departures include Keir Starmer's deputy PM, David Lammy., as well as Liz Kendall, Peter Kyle and Steve Reed. There was a return to the top table for Angela Rayner and Louise Haigh. Prime Minister Andy Burnham has spoken to French President Emmanuel Macron. A Downing Street spokeswoman said: “The Prime Minister began by setting out how he is looking forward to building on the strong UK-France relationship to deliver for people on both sides of the Channel. “Turning to Ukraine, the two leaders discussed the need to work together to continue supporting Ukraine as it resists Russia’s full-scale invasion, including through co-chairing the Coalition of the Willing. “On the Middle East, the two leaders agreed the need to continue efforts towards deescalation and to reopen the Strait of Hormuz. “They also discussed continuing the UK and France’s close co-operation on energy and defence, and the Prime Minister paid tribute to President Macron for his efforts to bring the Bayeux Tapestry to the UK, which underlined the UK and France’s enduring friendship. “The two leaders look forward to speaking again and the Prime Minister welcoming President Macron to the United Kingdom at the earliest opportunity.” John Healey has said the new Government led by Andy Burnham “will meet our commitments on defence to our international allies”. The new Chancellor was asked by broadcasters on Monday night if he would meet a commitment to spend 3% of national economic output on defence by 2030. Mr Healey expressed frustration that Sir Keir Starmer had not set out a clear timetable for the commitment when he resigned last month as defence secretary. Asked if welfare cuts or new taxes were on the cards to meet the goal, Mr Healey said: “Fiscal control is the first duty of any chancellor. It is mine. And fiscal credibility is the bedrock for economic stability and for national security. “You heard the Prime Minister this afternoon say, in this more dangerous world, we will meet our commitments on defence to our international allies.” Andy Burnham walked into Downing Street as Britain’s seventh Prime Minister since 2016. That fact alone tells the story of a country exhausted by political churn, broken promises and leaders who arrive declaring a fresh start before delivering more of the same. Mr Burnham knows it. To his credit, he said so. He admitted politics has not been good enough for the people it is meant to serve and spoke directly to families and pensioners who have spent years watching bills rise, wages struggle, and public services fray. That honesty matters. But honesty is only the beginning. The most important promise for readers may be his pledge to give people more breathing room on the cost of living, including considering a raise to the tax-free personal allowance.
