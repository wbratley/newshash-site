The new Prime Minister showed a glint of steel as he sacked Rachel Reeves, David Lammy, Lord Hermer and a string of his predecessor's closest Cabinet supporters within hours of taking office. In a move that stunned Westminster, he installed former defence secretary John Healey as Chancellor, confounding weeks of speculation that the job would go to Ed Miliband or Shabana Mahmood. Angela Rayner was reappointed Housing Secretary – ten months after she was forced out over an unpaid stamp duty bill. Today Mr Burnham will try to head off calls for a general election by unveiling immediate plans to ease the cost of living. He said cuts to energy bills, a £2 cap on bus fares and even rent controls were being looked at as he tries to make an immediate impact with a sceptical public after seizing power by stealth. He said any new measures would be fully funded, raising fears he will hike taxes on the middle classes. Mr Burnham boasted he would 'bring back hope' and introduce the 'biggest changes in the last 40 years – a new political model and a new economic model'. Writing in today's Daily Mail, Kemi Badenoch accused Labour of plunging Britain into a 'reckless experiment' which would 'inevitably run aground'. She urged Mr Burnham to cut welfare instead of imposing 'more Labour tax hikes'. Nigel Farage called for an immediate election. Also writing in this newspaper, he said the way Mr Burnham had 'crowbarred his way into No 10' was 'deeply troubling'. Mr Miliband, who played a key role in the coup against Sir Keir, was rewarded with promotion to Foreign Secretary. Former health secretary Wes Streeting was made Defence Secretary, while Lisa Nandy retained her job as Culture Secretary. Yvette Cooper was demoted to Health Secretary despite pleading to stay on to provide continuity at the Foreign Office, which has now churned through ten foreign secretaries in as many years. Ms Mahmood remains as Home Secretary after watering down plans to make migrants wait longer to get the right to live in the UK. Mr Burnham becomes the seventh prime minister in a turbulent political decade. The new PM, who was part of the coup against Sir Keir, said it was time for his generation of politicians to 'raise our game' and 'show the world that we can regain our stability again'. When he became Labour leader on Friday, he insisted that he had a plan for the country. Yesterday he admitted that it remained a work in progress, saying his ten-year vision will be published in the autumn. Mr Burnham ruled out holding a general election, despite having the weakest mandate of any modern PM. He did not stand on Labour's election-winning manifesto, he faced no leadership contest and the 25,000 voters who backed him in Makerfield represent just 0.05 per cent of the electorate. But the new premier insisted he has 'the legitimacy to lead'. But yesterday he said: 'We're in a parliamentary democracy and, as we've seen through time, if somebody can command the majority of the MPs in the House of Commons, obviously they then have the legitimacy to lead things forward.' In a short speech in Downing Street, he pledged to 'bring back hope', but gave few details. He made no mention of immigration, crime or foreign affairs. Mr Miliband is likely to prove a controversial choice as Foreign Secretary. The Trump administration is reported to have warned that appointing him to the top diplomatic role would be 'a mistake'.
