Out of this world! Astrophotography awards winners are revealed

Article text

Out of this world! Astrophotography awards winners are revealed - See more Daily Mail on Google - save us as a Preferred Source The winners of this year's international astrophotography awards have been revealed, with stunning images taken across the world wowing judges. The awe-inspiring photos were taken as part of the David Malin Awards, which showcase the finest images taken of the night sky. The awards, named after British-Australian astronomer and astrophotographer David Frederick Malin, are run by the Central West Astronomical Society in New South Wales, Australia. Malin, now 85, is known for discovering the gigantic spiral galaxy - aptly named Malin 1 - in 1986, as well as his colour images and photographic processing techniques of astronomical objects. Malin used giant telescopes at the Anglo-Australian Observatory in New South Wales to develop ground-breaking new photographic techniques which allowed astronomers to better decipher galactic distance, movement, structure and elements. The winners of this year's awards certainly applied those techniques - with incredible results. The competition was held as part of Central West's annual festival of astronomy, aka AstroFest. It invited amateur astronomers and photographers from around the world to take part and submit their photographs for consideration in the prestigious awards. The winner of the Wide-Field category was Jarrod Koh's 'The Dividing Line', featuring the Orion Molecular Cloud Complex Brighton Seeley's Zenith won the Nightscapes category. The stunning image, taken from a beach, captures the Aurora Australis in vibrant red and green Stefan Buda's The Lunar Apennines won the Overall winner and Solar System winner category. The image, illuminated through fine shades of grey, even shows the site of the Apollo 15 landing Luke Tscharke's Tasman Arc, showing the Aurora Aurealis sweeping across Tasman Island, got an honourable mention in the Nightscapes category Shooting a star! Murray Parkinson's 2025 R3 PanSTARRS was cleverly created using 45 one-minute exposures aligned separately on the stars and the comet, then recombining the two images to create a realistic shooting comet This stunning sunrise image by Ed Hurst, taken in the world's second largest canyon, shows a sky full of star trails. 'Pantoneys Crown' got an honourable mention in the Nightscapes category Andy Campbell's 'Winged Sandals of Mercury' showed an incredible structure within the Vela supernova remnant, receiving an honourable mention in the Deep Sky category This image, which looks more like a mythical creature than a photo of the night sky, shows the star-making region within the Puppis constellation, which is about 1,300 light years from earth. Frank Alvaro titled it 'God's Hand' and it received an honourable mention in the Deep Sky category Jarrod Rueff's 'Bran 56' got an honourable mention in the Deep Sky category. Looking like an emerging sea wave, it actually captures the nebulous region, BRAN 56 This biblical image, taken by Luke Rasmussen and titled 'The Wishing Tree', captured an aurora australis on Tasmania's Bruny Island Rodney Watters' image, which looks like a Rembrandt painting of a flame, captures the nebula Gum12b in the southern hemisphere constellations of Antlia and Vela. It won an honourable mention in the Wide-Field category Andy Campbell's The Rippling Flames - Chamaeleon III: Deep Sky won the Deep Sky category. The beautiful image captured the Chamaeleon molecular cloud littered with stars This ethereal image taken by Elizabeth Armarego won an honourable mention in the Deep Sky category. Titled 'Celestial Dolphin', it captured a doubly ionised oxygen object, formed by intense stellar winds The judges for the category winners this year were Peter Ward, Alex Cherney and Selena Simpson. The Photo Editor's Choice was judged by Nicola Amoros. The winning images were truly breathtaking, with landscapes of the solar system, Orion's belt, stars and the atmosphere being illuminated in high definition. Stefan Buda's 'The Lunar Apennines' won the overall winner category, depicting lunar surface in grey and white colours. The image of the lunar Apennine mountain region appears as though it was photographed from lunar orbit, not Earth. The winning image is in such high definition that the site of the Apollo 15 landing is visible. Other highlights include Andy Campbell's 'The Rippling Flames - Chamaeleon III', winner of the Deep Sky category. Campbell's image appears more like a painting than a photograph, depicting in greys, browns, blues and purples an incredible array of stars illuminating the Chamaeleon molecular cloud. Brighton Seeley's 'Zenith', winner of the Nightscapes (International) category, is perhaps the most colourful of all, depicting a high geomagnetic storm above a beach on earth. Seeley captured the beautiful display of the Aurora Australis in vibrant green and red light, reaching to the zenith. The winners of the prestigious awards receive trophies and a share of a prize pool including premium photography and imaging gear. The best entries also earn professional printing and year-long public exhibition at the Parkes Observatory, plus a nationwide touring exhibition.
