Furious Trump promises payback 'many times over' for each American soldier killed in war against Iran as US launches strikes for 10th consecutive night - See more Daily Mail on Google - save us as a Preferred Source President Donald Trump vowed payback for the deaths of each American soldier killed in the war against Iran as the United States began launching missiles at the country for a 10th consecutive night. The ongoing war in the Middle East has already killed 17 US servicemembers, including First Lieutenant Tyler James Feehan, 25, and Private Isabella Gonzales, 19, who died in an attack on an American air base in Jordan on Friday. A third US soldier was killed in northern Iraq on Saturday during the controlled detonation of an unexploded Iranian drone. The Pentagon has not released the soldier's identity. Central Command is also working to establish whether remains found close to the devastated Jordan base belong to a fourth service member who had been listed as missing. The deaths have now drawn President Trump's ire. 'Every time Iran kills an American Soldier they will pay for that killing many times over!' he posted to his Truth Social page Monday evening. He added that the directive 'has been passed on to Secretary of War Pete Hegseth, Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff, Daniel Caine, and every Leader in the Military.' Soon after, US Central Command announced it was conducting another wave of airstrikes on Iranian military assets in an effort to reopen the Strait of Hormuz, through which one-fifth of the world's oil supply traverses. CENTCOM said on social media that the latest strikes 'are designed to further degrade Iranian military capabilities used to attack commercial shipping' in the strait. The country's Islamic Revolutionary Guard later said it targeted a 'US HIMARS system' stationed at Camp Arifjan in Kuwait with ground-to-ground missiles. In a statement, the army said the attack was part of the 18th phase of its Thunder operation, and was launched in response to the US strikes on Iran. The US and Iran have been volleying missiles at each other for more than a week, after the US accused Iran of violating a Memorandum of Understanding to end the war by attacking oil tankers passing through the Persian Gulf. American forces are now seeking to cripple Tehran's ability to strike commercial shipping tanks while the US Navy reimposed a blockade on all of the Iranian regime's ports and seized several of its ships. 'The US is planning for a wider war,' said one American official familiar to the Washington Post, noting that the Pentagon was boosting the number of aircraft in the region. The official cautioned that the expanded operation would be limited because US stocks of air-defense and long-range weapons are running low, and battle damage was restricting how many extra troops and warplanes can be funneled into the region. Previous strikes overnight on Sunday had also targeted Iranian command centers, air defenses, naval assets, coastal surveillance posts and missile, drone and communications sites. At least one person was killed around Tabiz, a northwestern city about 325 miles from Tehran that is believed to host underground missile bases run by the Islamic Regime's Revolutionary Guard. The British military's United Kingdom Maritime Trade Operations center also announced Monday evening that a second ship had been attacked on Sunday in the Strait of Hormuz, off the coast of the United Arab Emirates. The vessel caught fire in the Strait of Hormuz after being hit by a projectile near the coastline of Oman. The crew abandoned the vessel, which was adrift and still ablaze hours later, the UKMTO said. The route around Oman has been the one the US military has encouraged ships to travel to avoid Iran's control. The Guard later claimed it was targeting tankers in the strait. Tensions may now escalate even further as Iran-backed Houthi rebels in Yemen threatened on Monday to prevent Saudi Arabia from shipping in the Red Sea, an alternate route for oil exports during the war. The Houthis, who are backed by Iran, said they would block shipping between the Red Sea and the Gulf of Aden in response to an attack on Sanaa International Airport last week that they blamed on Saudi Arabia. With the Strait of Hormuz blocked up, Saudi Arabia has been relying on a pipeline to the Red Sea to get millions of barrels of oil out to market. Fireworks burst as Houthi supporters rallied against the Saudi-led coalition in Sanaa, Yemen But the ongoing strikes have already pushed oil prices higher across the world. Benchmark Brent crude traded Monday above $88 a barrel and regular gasoline in the US climbed to an average of $4 a gallon, keeping pressure on Americans' wallets ahead of midterm elections this fall. Yet there was a glimmer of hope as Pakistan intensifies its diplomatic efforts to resuscitate the interim ceasefire reached last month, which called for resolving remaining issued ties to the war within 60 days. Iranian Interior Minister Eskandar Momeni arrived in Islamabad on Monday for two days of talks with Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif and others. Momeni's counterpart, Pakistani Interior Minister Mohsin Naqvi, expressed optimism. 'God willing, we will have good news,' Naqvi said. On Sunday, US Secretary of State Marco Rubio also told reporters that the country is still open to negotiating with Iran but that 'it has to be real.' 'If the door opens to diplomacy - if the guys that want to do something productive for Iran win and take control of that system, or take control of the negotiations - that´ll be a very positive development,' Rubio said. 'That´s not where we are tonight, unfortunately.'
